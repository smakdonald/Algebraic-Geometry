I’ll just prove the second one: If \(P \in V(I) \cup V(J)\) then for all \(f \in I\text{,}\) \(g \in J\) we have \((fg)(P) = f(P) g(P) = 0\) since either \(f(P) = 0\) or \(g(P) = 0\text{.}\) Thus \(P \in V(\{f g \mid f \in I, g \in J\}) = V(IJ)\text{,}\) where the last equality uses Proposition 1.1.13.
Say \(P \notin V(I) \cup V(J)\text{.}\) Then for any \(f \in I, g \in J'\text{,}\) we have \(f(P) \ne 0\) and \(g(P) \ne 0\) and thus \((fg)(P) = f(P) g(P) \ne 0\text{.}\) This proves \(P \notin V(\{f g \mid f \in I, g \in J\}) = V(IJ)\text{.}\)
