    Section 1.5 Regular Functions

    Definition 1. The dimension of a noetherian topological space \(X\text{,}\) written \(\operatorname{dim}(X) \in \{0, 1, 2, \dots, \infty\}\text{,}\) is the supremum of the set of all integers \(n \geq 0\) such that there exists a chain
    \begin{equation*} \emptyset \subsetneq Y_0 \subsetneq Y_1 \subsetneq \cdots \subsetneq Y_n \subseteq X \end{equation*}
    with each \(Y_i\) an irreducible closed subset of \(X\text{.}\) (The dimension of the empty set is \(-\infty\text{.}\) It is possible that the dimension of a noetherian space is infinite.)
    Given a closed irreducible subset \(Y\) of \(X\text{,}\) the codimension of \(Y\) in \(X\text{,}\) written \(\operatorname{codim}_X(Y)\text{,}\) the supremum over all \(n\) such that a choice of the form
    \begin{equation*} Y = Y_0 \subsetneq Y_1 \subsetneq \cdots \subsetneq Y_n \subseteq X. \end{equation*}
    Remark 1. Note that we only define codimension when \(Y\) is irreducible. When \(Y\) is reducible, sometimes one defines \(\operatorname{codim}_X(Y)\) to be the minimum value of \(\operatorname{codim}_X(Y')\) where \(Y'\) ranges over the irreducible components of \(Y\text{.}\)
    Remark 1. Topologize \(\mathbb{N}\) by declaring a subset to be closed if and only if it is of the form \(\{0, 1, \dots, n\}\) for some \(-1 \leq n \leq \infty\text{.}\) Then \(\mathbb{N}\) is a noetherian space but has infinite dimension. There exist noetherian commutative rings \(R\) such that \(\operatorname{dim}\operatorname{Spec}(R) = \infty\text{.}\) (This cannot occur for local rings nor for rings that are finitely generated as \(k\)-algebras.)
    Remark 1. Observe that every maximal chain of irreducible closed subsets (i.e., every chain that cannot be made longer and inserting new terms) of \(X\) has \(Y_n\) equal to an irreducible component of \(X\) and has \(Y_0\) equal to a single point. In particular,
    \begin{equation*} \operatorname{dim}(X) = \max \{\operatorname{dim}(Y) \mid \text{$Y$ is an irreducible component of $X$}\} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \operatorname{dim}(X) = \max \{\operatorname{codim}_X(P) \mid \text{$P$ is any point of $X$}\}. \end{equation*}
    Example 1. Let \(X = V(xy,xz)\text{,}\) the union of the \(x\)-axis and the \(yz\)-plane. The dimension of \(X\) is two. It is clearly at least two, since we have the chain with \(Y_0\) being any point on the \(yz\)-plane, \(Y_1\) being any line in this plane and containing this point, and \(Y_2\) being the whole plane. It is harder to see that it is at most two. This is an example of a variety that fails to be ‚Äúequi-dimensional‚Äù ‚Äî the variety has two irreducible components, but they have different dimensions.
    Let \(Y = V(x) \subseteq X\text{,}\) the \(yz\)-plane. Then \(\operatorname{codim}_X(Y) = 0\) since the only closed subsets strictly between \(Y\) and \(X\) are given by \(Y\) together with a finite set of points on the line.
    Let \(Z = V(y,z)\text{,}\) the \(x\)-axis. Then \(\operatorname{codim}_X(Z)\) is also \(0\) by similar reasoning. Since \(\operatorname{dim}(Z) = 1\text{,}\) this is a bit counter-intuitive.
    Let \(W = V(x,y,z)\text{.}\) When \(\operatorname{codim}_X(W) = 2\text{.}\) It‚Äôs at least two since we have the chain \(W \subsetneq V(x,y) \subsetneq Y \subseteq X\text{;}\) it is less obvious, but true, that it is exactly two.

    The following result follows from the fact that there is an order-reversing bijections between primes ideals of \(A(X)\) and irreducible closed subsets of \(X\text{:}\)
    Proposition 1. The dimension of an affine variety \(X\) is equal to the Krull dimension of its coordinate ring \(A(X)\text{.}\) The codimension of an irreducible closed subset \(Y\) of \(X\) is the height of the corresponding prime ideal of \(A(X)\text{.}\)
    Remark 1. Using the definition in Remark [cross-reference to target(s) "rem131" missing or not unique], the codimension of an arbitrary closed subset \(Y\) is the height of the corresponding radical ideal.
    Theorem 1 (Properties of dimension). Assume \(k\) is algebraically closed and \(X\) and \(Y\) are irreducible affine \(k\)-varieties
    1. We have \(\operatorname{dim}(X \times Y) = \operatorname{dim}(X) + \operatorname{dim}(Y)\) (see the problem set for the definition of \(X \times Y\)).
    2. (Catenary property) If \(Y \subseteq X\) then \(\operatorname{dim}(X) = \operatorname{dim}(Y) + \operatorname{codim}_X(Y)\text{.}\)
    3. (Krull‚Äôs Theorem). If \(f\) is a non-zero, non-unit element of \(A(X)\) (i.e. \(\emptyset \subsetneq V_X(f) \subsetneq X\)), then every irreducible component of \(V_X(f)\) has codimension \(1\) in \(X\) (or, equivalently thanks to (b), has dimension \(\operatorname{dim}(X) - 1\)). In particular, \(\operatorname{dim}V_X(f) = \operatorname{dim}(X) - 1\text{.}\)
    All of these results are best proven in a class on commutative algebra, and so I‚Äôll just skip their proofs.

    A special case of the Catenary Property is:
    Corollary 1. For every point \(P\) of an irreducible variety \(X\text{,}\) we have \(\operatorname{codim}_X(P) = \operatorname{dim}(X)\text{.}\)
    The previous result says that the dimension of an irreducible variety is that same near every point. In algebra terms, it says that if \(R\) is an integral domain and is a finitely generated \(k\)-algebra, then \(\operatorname{dim}R_{\mathfrak m}\) is the same for all maximal ideals \({\mathfrak m}\text{.}\)
